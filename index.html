BioWave™ Stress Regulation Study

This internal exploratory human-factors study was conducted to evaluate the effectiveness of the BioWave™ NeuroHaptic™ system in accelerating physiological recovery from acute stress among college students. The study focused on autonomic nervous system activity using Heart Rate Variability (HRV) as the primary biomarker.

Study Type Within-Subject
Participants 45 Students
Age Range 18–24 yrs
Primary Biomarker HRV

1. Study Design

A within-subject comparative design was employed, wherein each participant served as their own control. This approach was selected to reduce inter-individual variability in baseline stress reactivity, cardiovascular tone, and emotional regulation capacity.

Each participant completed the same stress-induction protocol under two conditions:

  1. Baseline condition: No wearable intervention.
  2. BioWave™ condition: NeuroHaptic™ stimulation enabled.

2. Participant Characteristics

The study included 45 undergraduate students recruited from academic institutions. Participation was voluntary.

  • Gender: Mixed (not stratified)
  • Health status: Self-reported healthy individuals
  • Inclusion criteria: Enrolled students aged 18–24
  • Exclusion criteria: Known cardiovascular, neurological, or autonomic disorders; use of beta-blockers or autonomic-altering medication

3. Stress Induction Protocol

Acute stress was induced using a standardized cognitive stress task designed to simulate academic examination pressure. The protocol included:

  • Timed mental arithmetic problems
  • Working-memory challenges
  • Countdown pressure with auditory cues

This protocol reliably elicits sympathetic nervous system activation, reflected by reduced HRV and increased cardiovascular arousal.

4. Measurement & Instrumentation

Physiological data were collected using a wrist-based photoplethysmography (PPG) sensor integrated into the BioWave™ prototype.

  • Sampling: Continuous HRV recording
  • Metric: Relative HRV change from stress peak
  • Interpretation: Higher HRV indicates stronger parasympathetic (vagal) activity and improved autonomic balance

5. BioWave™ Intervention

During the BioWave™ condition, HRV patterns were analyzed in real time. When sustained stress markers were detected, the system automatically initiated NeuroHaptic™ stimulation.

The stimulation consisted of low-frequency, rhythmic vibration patterns delivered to nerve-dense regions of the wrist. No breathing exercises, instructions, or conscious relaxation techniques were provided to participants.

6. Outcome Measures

  • Time to autonomic recovery (minutes)
  • Percentage HRV improvement from stress peak
  • Self-reported perceived calmness post-task

7. Results

Average HRV increased by 22% within the first 3 minutes of BioWave™ activation compared with baseline.

  • 38% faster autonomic recovery relative to baseline condition
  • 87% of participants showed measurable physiological improvement
  • 62% reported noticeable calming within 2 minutes

NeuroHaptic™ stimulation enabled with adaptive intensity.

8. Scientific Interpretation

Increased HRV reflects enhanced parasympathetic engagement and reduced sympathetic dominance. The accelerated recovery observed in the BioWave™ condition suggests that the system actively supports intrinsic stress-regulation mechanisms rather than passively monitoring stress.

This evaluation was short-term, non-clinical, and limited to a student population. The study was not powered for clinical inference. BioWave™ is not a medical device and does not diagnose or treat disease. No personally identifiable data were stored.

10. Conclusion

The BioWave™ NeuroHaptic™ system demonstrated rapid, consistent, and measurable improvements in physiological stress recovery under controlled academic stress conditions, supporting its potential as a practical calm-tech intervention for student populations.

How BioWave™ Works

  • Predictive HRV Engine: Detects rising stress patterns.
  • NeuroHaptic™ Feedback: Parasympathetic stimulation via wrist nerves.
  • Closed-loop Regulation: Adaptive response modulation.
